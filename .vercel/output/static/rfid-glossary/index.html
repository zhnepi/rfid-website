RFID Glossary

A comprehensive reference for RFID and NFC terminology

Navigate essential concepts, standards, chip families and system components. Use the alphabetical index to find definitions quickly.

A B C D E F H I L M N R T U

A

Antenna

The component in an RFID system that transmits radio frequency signals from the reader and receives signals from the tag. Reader antennas radiate RF energy, while tag antennas receive and transmit data back to the reader.

Anti-collision

The ability of a reader to identify and process data from multiple RFID tags simultaneously without data corruption. Anti-collision algorithms allow the reader to manage signals from multiple tags in the same field.

B

Backscatter

The method by which a passive RFID tag reflects radio waves back to the reader to transmit data. The tag modulates the reflected signal to encode information, consuming minimal power since it requires no internal battery.

C

Chip Memory (EEPROM)

Electrically Erasable Programmable Read-Only Memory used in RFID chips to store data. EEPROM allows data to be written and erased multiple times, making it suitable for rewritable RFID cards and tags.

Contactless Smart Card

A card containing an embedded RFID chip that communicates with a reader without physical contact. Commonly used for access control, payment systems, hotel key cards and identification credentials.

Coupling (Inductive vs Electromagnetic)

The method of energy transfer between reader and tag. Inductive coupling is used in low and high frequency RFID, while electromagnetic coupling is used in UHF systems. Coupling efficiency affects read range and performance.

D

DESFire

NXP's high-security contactless smart card IC offering advanced encryption and authentication capabilities. DESFire chips support 3DES and AES encryption, making them suitable for secure applications requiring data protection.

E

Encoding

The process of writing data to an RFID chip's memory. Encoding can occur during manufacturing or at any point in the tag's lifecycle, allowing for customization, numbering and personalization of products.

EPC (Electronic Product Code)

A standardized global identifier system for RFID tags that uniquely identifies products in supply chain and retail applications. EPC Gen2 is the most common standard, enabling interoperability across industries.

F

Frequency Hopping

A technique where an RFID system rapidly changes transmission frequency across a defined range to reduce interference and improve reliability. This is particularly useful in environments with competing RF signals.

H

HF (High Frequency)

RFID operating frequency range of 13.56 MHz. HF systems offer moderate read range (up to several meters) and are commonly used for NFC, hotel key cards, library tags and access control applications.

I

Inlay

A pre-assembled component consisting of an RFID chip and antenna laminated or bonded together. Inlays serve as the core building block for manufacturing RFID labels, cards and tags.

ISO 14443

An international standard for contactless smart cards defining two types: Type A and Type B. ISO 14443 cards operate at 13.56 MHz and are widely used for payment cards, access control and identification.

ISO 15693

An international standard for vicinity cards operating at 13.56 MHz. ISO 15693 cards have a longer read range than ISO 14443 (up to 1 meter) and are used for library systems, healthcare and inventory applications.

ISO 18000-6C / EPC Gen2

An international standard defining the air interface for UHF RFID systems. EPC Gen2 is the global standard for supply chain and logistics, enabling read ranges up to 10+ meters with anti-collision capability.

L

LF (Low Frequency)

RFID operating frequency range of 125 kHz (and nearby bands). LF systems offer short read range (typically under 0.5 meters) but excellent penetration through metal and liquids, ideal for animal tracking and access control.

M

Middleware

Software layer that bridges RFID readers and backend business applications. Middleware handles data filtering, validation, routing and integration, translating raw tag data into meaningful business information.

MIFARE

NXP's family of contactless smart card technology based on ISO 14443 Type A. MIFARE includes Classic, Plus and DESFire variants, widely used in transportation, access control and payment systems worldwide.

N

NDEF (NFC Data Exchange Format)

A standardized message format for exchanging data between NFC-enabled devices. NDEF allows NFC tags to store and share various data types such as text, URLs, contact information and configuration instructions.

NFC (Near Field Communication)

A short-range wireless technology enabling data exchange between two devices (typically a phone and a tag) at frequencies of 13.56 MHz. NFC requires close proximity (usually under 10 centimeters) for communication.

NTAG

NXP's family of NFC tag ICs optimized for simple, low-cost applications. NTAG chips support NDEF format and are commonly used in NFC stickers, wristbands and interactive labels for smartphones.

R

Read Range

The maximum distance at which an RFID reader can successfully communicate with a tag. Read range varies by frequency (LF: <0.5m, HF: 0-1m, UHF: 1-10m+), antenna design and environmental conditions.

Reader/Writer

An RFID device that transmits RF energy to tags and reads data returned by them. Readers can also encode or write data to tags in real-time, functioning as both read and write devices in RFID systems.

RFID (Radio Frequency Identification)

A wireless technology that uses radio waves to automatically identify and track objects. RFID systems consist of a reader, antenna and tags that contain microchips and store data.

T

Tag Sensitivity

The minimum RF signal strength required for an RFID tag to respond to a reader. Higher sensitivity allows tags to communicate from greater distances and in challenging environments, improving overall system performance.

Transponder

The RFID tag or chip unit that receives signals from a reader and transmits data back. A transponder consists of a microchip and antenna, functioning as the primary data carrier in an RFID system.

U

UHF (Ultra High Frequency)

RFID operating frequency range of 860–960 MHz. UHF systems offer the longest read range (up to 10+ meters), highest data transfer speeds and are the standard for supply chain, logistics and inventory management.

UID (Unique Identifier)

A unique serial number permanently programmed into each RFID chip at manufacture. The UID cannot be changed and serves as the primary identifier for the tag in most applications and systems.
